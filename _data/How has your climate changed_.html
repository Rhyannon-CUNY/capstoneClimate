How has your climate changed?

Winters are warmer, summers are longer and disasters are happening more often. Here's what you can expect.

Published [Date]

My grandma turned 100-years-old this year. She was born a year before Marilyn Monroe.

Summers are so hot now that she can't leave the house. There were a record 23 days above 95°F this year in Tokyo. 100,000 people were taken to hospital for heatstroke.

No days were that hot when she was growing up.

The climate has changed a lot in her lifetime, but how much has it changed since you were born?

What will it be like when you are 100?

Spring

Spring is starting 4 days earlier than it did when you were born. Sweater weather is becoming shorter.

The average temperature was 37.6°F the year you were born.

These days it's 41.1°F.

In 1925, 100 years ago, the average temperature was 37.5°F

Somewhere like Nebraska has experienced more change: from 44.4°F to 50.9°F since you were born.

Summer

The average temperature was 64.2°F when you were born.

One hundred years ago, the average temperature was 61.8°F.

Nevada has had more noticeable change since you were born: from 65.6°F to 71.8°F.

Fall

Winter is starting 5 days later than it used to. It's warmer for longer.

The average temperature was 44.7°F when you were born.

Going back 100 years, the average was 43.1°F.

Some states have changed more. In Texas fall temperatures have risen from 65.2°F to 70.6°F since you were born.

Winter

129 days were below freezing when you were born.

Because winters are getting warmer, now only 111 are that cold.

Winter cooled to 3.6°F when you were born.

These days it only reaches 9.6°F.

One hundred years ago, it was as cold as 3.4°F.

In Vermont though, winters have warmed more since you were born. The coldest temperatures went from 4.2°F to 10.8°F.

Your future

There'll be 111 days of extreme cold when you reach 100.

For someone born in 2000, there'll be 2 extremely hot days by the time they turn 100.

For a 25 year old now, there'll only be 84 freezing days by the time they are 100.

Data Sources

This analysis is based on climate projections and historical data from the following sources:

NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information for climate data and temperature records, EPA Climate Indicators for Seasonal timing and frost data, University of Chicago Climate Impact Lab for Extreme heat and cold day projections.

Projections represent mid-range climate scenarios and actual outcomes may vary. The 1925 average temperature is based on a 30-year seasonal average from 1895-1925.
